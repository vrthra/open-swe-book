# Chapter 2 — Open Resources

Free, open‑licensed, or freely accessible material for going deeper on software
development processes. Legend: 📘 book/book chapter · 🎓 course/courseware · 📄 paper or
standard · 🎥 video/talk. Licenses vary and are noted where known.

## Foundational values

- 📄 **Manifesto for Agile Software Development** — the four values and twelve principles that
  define the agile movement, plus the list of original signatories.
  <https://agilemanifesto.org/> · Free to read; the manifesto text is freely reproducible with
  attribution to its authors.

- 📄 **The Scrum Guide (2020)**, Ken Schwaber and Jeff Sutherland — the definitive, vendor-
  neutral 13-page definition of Scrum's accountabilities, events, and artifacts; the site's
  revision history documents the changes from earlier editions.
  <https://scrumguides.org/scrum-guide.html> · Released under Creative Commons
  Attribution-ShareAlike (CC BY-SA 4.0).

- 📘 **Extreme Programming**, Portland Pattern Repository (C2 wiki) — the original community
  wiki where XP's practices (TDD, pair programming, simple design, continuous integration) were
  discussed and refined. <http://wiki.c2.com/?ExtremeProgramming> · Free to read.

- 📘 **The Art of Unix Programming**, Eric S. Raymond (2003) — chapter 1 collects the Unix
  philosophy's maxims, including Doug McIlroy's "do one thing well" dicta and the rule that
  clarity is better than cleverness. <http://www.catb.org/esr/writings/taoup/html/> · Free to
  read online (full text posted by the author).

- 📘 **Extreme Programming Explained: Embrace Change (2nd ed.)**, Kent Beck with Cynthia
  Andres (2004) — XP's values and practices from their originator; the primary source for
  §2.3. <https://www.informit.com/store/extreme-programming-explained-embrace-change-9780321278654>
  · Commercial book (publisher page); widely held in libraries.

- 📘 **Test-Driven Development: By Example**, Kent Beck (2002) — the red–green–refactor loop
  worked through complete examples; the primary source for TDD in §2.3.2.
  <https://www.informit.com/store/test-driven-development-by-example-9780321146533> ·
  Commercial book (publisher page); widely held in libraries.

- 📄 **"Essential XP: Card, Conversation, Confirmation"**, Ron Jeffries (2001) — the classic
  "three Cs" statement of what a user story is (and is not); companion to Alistair Cockburn's
  "promise for a conversation."
  <https://ronjeffries.com/xprog/articles/expcardconversationconfirmation/> · Free to read.

- 📄 **"INVEST in Good Stories, and SMART Tasks"**, Bill Wake (2003) — the article that coined
  the INVEST checklist for user stories used in §2.3.1 and Chapter 3.
  <https://xp123.com/articles/invest-in-good-stories-and-smart-tasks/> · Free to read.

- 📄 **"Continuous Integration"**, Martin Fowler (2024 revision) — the standard description of
  CI as an XP-born practice: integrate at least daily, verify every merge with an automated
  build and tests. <https://martinfowler.com/articles/continuousIntegration.html> · Free to
  read.

- 📄 **"Yagni"**, Martin Fowler (2015) — a careful unpacking of "you aren't gonna need it" and
  its relationship to XP's simple-design practice.
  <https://martinfowler.com/bliki/Yagni.html> · Free to read.

## Plan-driven and risk-driven models (primary sources)

- 📄 **"Managing the Development of Large Software Systems"**, Winston W. Royce (1970) — the
  paper universally cited as the origin of the "waterfall" diagram. Read it carefully: Royce
  presents the strict sequential model and then argues it is *risky* and must be iterated,
  which is the opposite of how it is usually invoked.
  <https://www.cs.umd.edu/class/spring2003/cmsc838p/Process/waterfall.pdf> · Free to read
  (archival scan mirrored on university course pages).

- 📄 **"A Spiral Model of Software Development and Enhancement"**, Barry W. Boehm (1988) — the
  original, highly readable presentation of the risk-driven spiral framework, from IEEE
  *Computer* 21(5). <https://www.cse.msu.edu/~cse435/Homework/HW3/boehm.pdf> · Free to read
  (university course mirror).

- 📄 **"Software Risk Management: Principles and Practices"**, Barry W. Boehm (1991) — defines
  risk exposure as probability times loss and lays out the identify–analyze–respond cycle
  behind §2.6, from IEEE *Software* 8(1).
  <https://www.cs.virginia.edu/~sherriff/papers/Boehm%20-%201991.pdf> · Free to read
  (university course mirror).

- 📄 **"Software Defect Reduction Top 10 List"**, Barry Boehm and Victor R. Basili (2001) — the
  concise empirical backing for the cost-of-change curve: finding and fixing a problem after
  delivery is often 100 times more expensive than during requirements and design, from IEEE
  *Computer* 34(1). <https://www.cs.umd.edu/projects/SoftEng/ESEG/papers/82.78.pdf> · Free to
  read (author-affiliated university copy).

- 📄 **"Things You Should Never Do, Part I"**, Joel Spolsky (2000) — the widely cited essay on
  the mid-1990s browser rewrite behind §2.6.3, and why rewriting working software from scratch
  concentrates risk.
  <https://www.joelonsoftware.com/2000/04/06/things-you-should-never-do-part-i/> · Free to
  read.

## Course material

- 📘🎓 **Engineering Software as a Service (ESaaS)**, Armando Fox and David Patterson, UC
  Berkeley — Chapter 7 covers agile, Scrum, and process in depth alongside a running project.
  Companion site with free materials, slides, and MOOC links. <https://www.saasbook.info/> ·
  Free companion materials; MOOCs offered free to audit on edX.

- 🎓 **MIT OpenCourseWare, 6.005 / 6.031 "Software Construction"** — open readings and
  problem sets on test-first development, specifications, and designing for change that
  complement this chapter's engineering practices. <https://ocw.mit.edu/> · Creative Commons
  Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike.

- 🎥 **"Agile Product Ownership in a Nutshell"**, Henrik Kniberg — a widely used 15-minute
  whiteboard talk explaining backlogs, prioritization, and the Product Owner role.
  Searchable on YouTube. · Free to view.

- 📘 **"Shape Up," Ryan Singer (Basecamp)** — the free online book behind §2.8: shaping,
  six‑week cycles plus a two‑week cool‑down, betting instead of backlogs, the circuit
  breaker, and hill charts. <https://basecamp.com/shapeup> · Free to read online.
