# Chapter 1 → Your Project: Form the Team, Find the Problems

> **Sprint alignment.** This chapter maps to **week one**, before Sprint 0 even begins.
> There is no backlog yet, no customer, no repository full of code — just a new team and a
> blank page. That is exactly when Chapter 1's big ideas (requirements are hard, scope is
> the only free variable, software can hurt people) do their cheapest work: before you have
> committed to anything. See
> [Running the Project on Two‑Week Sprints](../appendix-a-team-project/two-week-sprints.md).

## Both hats

The dual‑hat swap has not happened yet — you do not know whose idea you will build or who
will build yours. So this week everything you do serves both future hats at once.

1. **Write the team charter** ([Appendix A exercise 1](../appendix-a-team-project/exercises.md)):
   one paragraph on how you will communicate, how often you will meet, how you will make
   decisions, and what happens when a member falls behind. Commit it to your repository.
   Teams that skip this negotiate it later, mid‑crisis, at ten times the price.
2. **Start collecting pitch‑worthy problems — every member, individually.** Sprint 0‑0
   will ask each of you for at least one pitch, and the strongest pitches describe a
   **real, reachable user** with a problem you have actually observed, not a product you
   would enjoy building ([§1.2.1](./#121-identifying-users-and-requirements) — users come
   before requirements). Look at the jobs, clubs, and communities around you: who copes
   with something clumsy every week? Write each candidate down as *user + problem*, one
   sentence each.
3. **Attach a responsibility note to every candidate.** For each problem idea, answer
   §1.6's question before you fall in love with the solution: *who could this harm?* What
   data would it hold, who gets excluded if it misjudges its users, what happens when it
   fails ([§1.6](./#16-social-responsibility))? An idea whose honest answer scares you is
   an idea to fix or drop now — remember that the Therac‑25 and Volkswagen engineers also
   started with a reasonable‑sounding goal.
4. **Internalize the iron triangle** ([§1.5](./#15-balancing-constraints-the-iron-triangle)).
   Your entire term runs on fixed two‑week boxes with a fixed team: cost and time are
   constants, so **scope is the only variable you control**. Every planning conversation
   you will have this term is the triangle in miniature; teams that grasp this in week one
   cut scope calmly, and teams that don't cut sleep instead.

## Done means

- [ ] The team charter is written and committed to the repository.
- [ ] Every member has 2–3 candidate problems, each naming a specific, reachable real
      user and the one problem it solves.
- [ ] Every candidate carries a short responsibility note: who could this harm, and why
      you are comfortable proceeding anyway.
- [ ] The team can state, in its own words, why scope — not time, not effort — is the
      variable it will manage all term.

## Project exercises

_Do these as part of the sprint work above; they produce artifacts your sprint reports and
reviews can point to._

1. For your team‑project idea, list every distinct *stakeholder* (end users, secondary
   users, sponsors, regulators). For each, write one sentence on what they most need and
   one risk if that need is missed.
2. Name one requirement of your project that you expect to *change* during the term.
   Describe one *process‑level* and one *design‑level* way you will make that change
   cheap to accommodate.
3. Identify one way your project could cause real harm if it failed (privacy, safety,
   money, fairness). Write a one‑paragraph "responsibility note" for your team's README
   describing how you will guard against it.
